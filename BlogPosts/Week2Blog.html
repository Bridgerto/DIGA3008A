Section 1: ‘You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media (1991)’ by Stuart Moulthrop

This week’s reading by Stuart Moulthrop, You Say You Want a Revolution: Hypertext and the Laws of Media, really made me pause and think about the tech we use every day without questioning it. Moulthrop dives into the world of hypertext, you know, all those clickable links that send you bouncing from one page to the next, and asks: is this stuff truly revolutionary, or are we just dressing up old tools in a digital costume?

To help unpack that, Moulthrop uses Marshall McLuhan’s “Laws of Media,” a kind of four-part checklist to analyze what any new medium does to our world. The checklist goes like this: What does the medium enhance? What does it make old or useless? What does it bring back from the past? And finally, what happens when it’s pushed to the extreme? It’s kind of like looking at your favorite new gadget and figuring out its superpower, its weakness, its retro vibes, and what happens when it glitches. Moulthrop applies this to hypertext and makes one thing clear right off the bat: hypertext isn’t here to replace books. And honestly, that makes sense. Books are like a long walk on a quiet path, you go in one direction, slowly, thoughtfully. Hypertext, on the other hand, is more like zipping through a maze with twenty open tabs and YouTube playing in the background. It’s fast and flexible, sure, but it can also be pretty distracting.

While hypertext gives readers the power to jump between topics and explore freely, that same freedom can mess with our ability to sit with complex ideas. You don’t get the same kind of deep thinking when you’re constantly hopping from one link to another. Moulthrop warns that what once felt like a digital revolution might now just be a noisy echo of old media forms, just messier and more overwhelming. So is hypertext revolutionary or just... chaotic? Moulthrop doesn’t say it’s all bad. He just wants us to ask better questions about the tools we use. Because even though hypertext changed how we read, write, and think, it hasn’t made the book, or deep thought, obsolete. And maybe it never will.
