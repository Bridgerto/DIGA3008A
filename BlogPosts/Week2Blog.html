Question: Reflect on this week's reading by Moulthrop.
In You Say You Want a Revolution: Hypertext and the Laws of Media, Moulthrop speaks about the origin and evolution of hypertext. He offers an analysis on the role of hypertext in the digital culture by placing it against Marshall McLuhan’s Laws of Media which consists of his “four-part media interrogation” (Moulthrop 2003: P691). Here, Moulthroup looks at hypertext from the lens of McLuhan's question, all to assess whether hypertext is a revolutionary or problematic medium.

In fact, in the first paragraph he points out that “hypertext does not replace the book” - this speaks back to the role that each of these mediums play for cognitive and cultural abilities. Books are already pre-written meaning that an individual and deep and meaningful knowledge accumulate and sustain longer critical thoughts. Whereas hypertext is a form of linking branches of information, moving from one source of information to another breaks engagement and allows distractions to happen.
