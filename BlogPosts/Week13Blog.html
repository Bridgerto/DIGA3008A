The Internet as a Moral Space: Building with Care, Not Just Code

Week 13 Blog

Published by: Reneilwe Thobejane

Posted: 19th May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa
Last updated: 23rd May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa

What does an ethical internet look like to you?

The blog for today concerns a reading by Shani Ogrady. Her work speaks on an occurrence at Windsor Great Park, where Roger Silverstone spoke about his idea. Roger Silverstone was a theorist in Communication and Media, he spent a lot of time thinking about how we use the internet and media, like television and computers, to talk to each other and understand the world. One day, in a big, quiet park called Windsor Great Park, he gave a talk to a group of people. He said something very important: the internet isn't just a fun or useful plac, it's also a moral space. That means it's a place where we make choices about what's right and wrong. Shani Ogrady tells us about that day and about Silverstone's big idea. She explains that when we go online, like when we watch videos, send messages, or share pictures, we are not just pressing buttons. We are connecting with other people, and those people matter. Just like how we learn to be kind, take turns, and say sorry in real life, we need to learn how to do those things on the internet too.

One of Silverstone's most important thoughts was about a concept called “proper distance.” This means finding a good balance. We shouldn't be so far away that we don't care about others, but we also shouldn't be so close that we forget to respect their space. The internet makes it easy to see what's happening far away, but that doesn't mean we always understand or help in the right way. The author says we have to be careful and thoughtful when we use the internet. It's easy to hurt people with our words or ignore voices that matter. That's why she thinks we all need to learn how to be kind and responsible digital citizens.This idea connects with how we behave online. When we code, post, or share, we're not just pushing buttons. We're shaping people's experiences. Just like on a playground, we need to ask: “Am I being fair? Am I including others? Am I listening before I speak?” That's part of being a good digital citizen. It's about remembering that behind every screen is a real person with feelings, context, and identity.

But here's where things get deeper: Not everyone even has the same access to this moral space. And that's where digital inequality and digital decoloniality come in. When we talk about the internet as a space of connection, we also have to face the truth that many people are excluded from it, not just in terms of not having Wi-Fi, but in whose voices are heard, whose cultures are represented, and whose knowledge is taken seriously. Some communities don't have the tools, resources, or even the digital literacy to participate fully. Others are represented in ways that are biased, unfair, or stereotyped. Silverstone's idea reminds us that our responsibility online isn't just personal, it's also structural. We need to help build a more ethical internet where more people can participate meaningfully. That means pushing back against the “default” digital culture shaped by Western, corporate, often white and male perspectives. It means coding with care, designing websites that are accessible and culturally inclusive, and amplifying voices that have been historically silenced.

So digital decoloniality, to me, is also about proper distance, knowing when to speak, when to listen, and how to create space for others without overpowering or misrepresenting. It's about building an internet where connection doesn't just happen, but happens right. Where care, fairness, and equity guide not only how we act, but how we build the tools others use to act too.
