Coding With Care: Rethinking Design Through a Decolonial Lens

Week 12 Blog

Published by: Reneilwe Thobejane

Posted: 11th May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa
Last updated: 13th June, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa

Section 1: How we code matters - introducing Critical Code Literacies by Hanli Geyser

For this week’s blog, I will be speaking about Hanli’s Devconf Talk, where she challenges the myth that code is neutral or purely technical. She shows that people often talk about programming and treat it like it’s just math or logic (something neutral and objective). But coding is in fact actually a form of communication that is part of meaning-making. Just like how words carry meaning, emotion, and intention, so does the code that we write. When we write code, we’re not just solving problems, we are making choices. Every tiny decision, like what features to include, what data to ask for, how users interact with the site all reflects the way we have been taught to think. That way of thinking has a name: it’s called a paradigm, and it’s shaped by our beliefs, our education, and the systems we live in.

Often, we don’t even realize that we’ve been taught to think in ways that come from colonial systems (systems that made one way of knowing seem like the only “correct” or “valid” way). In the talk Hanli gives the example of with 2+2=, in one situation, it’s true. But change the context, and suddenly 4 squares arranged together might look like 5. Showing that how we answer questions depends on what we know, and what we know depends on how we’ve been taught to think. The same goes for how we write code: we make assumptions based on what we think is “normal” or “universal,” but those assumptions might exclude people with different experiences.

This lecture pushed me to start asking new questions when I code Because really, the goal it’s not just about being a good programmer. It’s about being a responsible one. Instead of just focusing on writing code that works, I need to focus on writing code that cares — code that includes more people, respects differences, and challenges those “default” settings that are rooted in colonial ways of thinking. So when I code my website, I need to look at:
Who is it for?
Who might be left out?
What ideas or biases am I bringing with me without even realizing?

And when I actually sit down and reflect, I see there are a lot of things I can do better. One of the first things is making sure my website works for people with disabilities, not just sighted, hearing, or fully able-bodied users. That means using semantic HTML so screen readers can properly read the content. It means checking that my color contrast is strong enough for people with low vision or color blindness. I also need to make sure that my site is navigable for people who can’t use a keyboard or mouse in the “normal” way. And of course, making sure it scales to any screen size because not everyone has a PC or laptop; many rely on phones or shared devices.

But even with all these accessibility fixes, there’s still the bigger issue of digital access itself. What about people who don’t have internet access at all? Or those who aren’t digitally literate? That’s something no amount of responsive design can fix, it reminds me that tech solutions can’t be separated from real-world inequality.

Another thing I’ve had to be honest about is how my project imposes a specific way of knowing. I’ve drawn a lot of inspiration from fashion and digital art websites, but most of those come from the global North. So, even without realizing, I’m importing a singular, Western aesthetic and knowledge system. I need to be more conscious of that. I’ll work on making my fonts, layouts, and visual metaphors more culturally sensitive, more globally accessible. So yep, this lecture helped me realize that code is not neutral. It’s communication. And the way I code tells a story, I just have to make sure it’s one that includes, not excludes.

Section 2: Reflection on the Reading - Algorithmic Colonization of Africa: Abeba Birhane

Abeba Birhane’s piece opened my eyes to how technology, especially systems like AI and algorithms, is not just about cool innovations — it’s deeply tied to power, control, and sometimes harm. Birhane talks about something called algorithmic colonization, which is basically when powerful tech companies from the West bring their tools and systems into African countries and say they are “helping”, but what they’re really doing is imposing their values and systems, just like colonial powers did in the past.

One example that really stuck with me was how companies use African countries to test out surveillance technology, or how they build facial recognition systems that can’t even recognize Black faces properly. Google's photo-tagging AI once labeled African-American faces as “gorillas” due to biased training data. It’s scary because these systems are being exported to countries that didn’t build them and don’t get a say in how they work. It’s like being forced to use a tool that wasn’t made for you and worse, one that could actually harm you.

The author explains that a lot of these technologies are being built using data from African people, but those same people don’t get to decide what happens with their data. It’s like someone coming into your house, taking your stuff, and then selling it back to you while pretending they are doing you a favor. But Birhane’s piece is not just doom and gloom. She talks about the importance of building systems that are grounded in community values, that respect context and culture. She calls for African voices to lead in tech conversations, not just be “included,” but actually be the ones shaping what ethical and fair technology looks like.

Section 3: My JavaScript Goals

I want my navigation system to be javascript based, to make it modular and less static.
My homepage will have a logo that expands and shrinks for dynamism.
I will have a return to top to bottom to make it hassle free for my users.
Then my website will be modular with I add a drop down menu from small screen sizes.
