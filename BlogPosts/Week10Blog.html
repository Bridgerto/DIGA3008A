Offline and Left Behind: Why the Internet Should Be a Human Right

Week 10 Blog

Published by: Reneilwe Thobejane

Posted: 12th May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa
Last updated: 13th June, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa

A Close Reading of Spyros Kasapis's “Internet Access is a Fundamental Right”

In his article “Internet Access is a Fundamental Right—Especially Amidst a Pandemic” (2020), Spyros Kasapis argues that internet access is no longer just a nice-to-have, it's something everyone should have as a basic right. He compares it to other essential needs like food, education, and healthcare. Writing during the COVID-19 pandemic, Kasapis shows how important the internet became for daily life, from working and learning at home to staying connected and informed. This essay takes a closer look at what Kasapis is saying, how he supports his ideas, and how his argument fits into wider discussions about digital inequality.

Right from the beginning, Kasapis is clear about what he wants readers to think about. He wants governments and the public to take internet access seriously, as something everyone deserves. He builds his case by referring to ideas about human dignity and happiness, even quoting Salvador Allende to show that people can't truly live full lives without access to things that help them participate in society. The pandemic, he explains, proved just how crucial the internet has become. It was the only way many people could attend school, go to work, talk to loved ones, or even see a doctor. For people without internet access, the lockdown was not just isolating, it cut them off from society completely.

Instead of using research data or statistics, Kasapis supports his argument with ethical reasoning and examples from the legal world. He mentions a 2020 court ruling in India that called internet access a constitutional right, and points out that international bodies like the United Nations are moving in a similar direction. This approach helps highlight the importance of the issue, but it also means the article misses some depth. For example, it doesn't explore how widespread internet access really is, or how affordability and infrastructure affect people in different parts of the world.

One of the strongest parts of the article is how it shows that the pandemic exposed deep inequalities. Kasapis says that being offline during the lockdown wasn't just frustrating, it was dangerous and unfair. This idea connects well with other writers like Nanjira Sambuli and Christoph Lutz, who point out that digital exclusion isn't just about having internet access, but also about being able to use it properly. People need good devices, enough data, digital skills, and the freedom to go online safely. While Kasapis hints at this, he doesn't fully explore how these issues play out across different groups, like low-income families, disabled people, or those living in rural areas.

Another area that feels a bit underdeveloped is the question of what should actually be done. Kasapis raises important points, should internet access be free? Who's responsible for making it happen?, but doesn't suggest specific solutions. He doesn't talk about how governments could work with internet providers or how public spaces like libraries could help. In comparison, some countries like Chile and Colombia took real steps during the pandemic by treating internet as a public utility. Thinkers like Merten Reglitz have also argued that internet access is needed to enjoy other rights, like education or political participation.

Kasapis's article also doesn't touch much on digital literacy. Just because someone has internet doesn't mean they can use it well or safely. Access is only the first step. People also need to know how to use digital tools, protect their privacy, and engage meaningfully online. This has been discussed by scholars like Jan van Dijk, who outlines that digital inclusion includes motivation, access, skills, and outcomes.

Despite these gaps, Kasapis's main point is powerful. He shows that being offline in today's world is more than inconvenient, it's unfair. The internet plays such a big role in our lives that denying people access widens existing inequalities. Recognizing internet access as a basic right could be a big step toward making society more equal.

In the end, Kasapis doesn't offer a full blueprint for how to achieve digital equity, but his message is clear and urgent. The pandemic showed us that digital access isn't a luxury anymore, it's a lifeline. His article pushes us to think about what kind of future we want, and how we can make sure that no one is left behind in a world that's increasingly online.
