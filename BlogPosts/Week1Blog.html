From First Push to Flash: A Journey Through the Web’s Past and My Digital Future.
Week 1 Blog

Published by: Reneilwe Thobejane

Posted: 18th February, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa
Lasted updated: 20th May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa

SECTION 1: Github Set-up

SETTING UP My Experience with setting up GitHub was paradoxically both simple and complicated. When the steps of setting it up in class were explained, it seemed straightforward. But sitting alone and attempting to do it was not a linear process. The first hurdle was realising that I misunderstood Visual Studios as Visual Studio Code. So you could imagine the frustration of staring at slides with VSCode having a blue interface while I had Visual Studios open and I kept seeing a purple interface. Eventually, I figured it out and downloaded VSCode.

From there, the next hiccup was misunderstanding one of the slides. After opening a new file in VSCode, and typing “index.html”. I was meant to type the exclamation mark (!), then click the tab button on the keyboard. But the slide did not explain it this way, it had written type !(tab). I was stuck on the step for a good day, but I reached out to my peers and got it sorted. From this point onwards, everything was smooth sailing. Overall, I would rate the experience a 7/10.

SECTION 2: The World-Wide Web (1994) by Tim Berners-Lee et al.

I chose an article by Tim Berners-Lee published in 1994, titled The World-Wide Web. This written piece introduces the origin of the WWW or, as the article refers to it, W3 which was initially created at CERN to help researchers manage and circulate information more efficiently. What stood out to me was how something so embedded in our everyday lives came from such a practical need: making knowledge more accessible within a specialized academic community.

Berners-Lee dives deep into the technical foundation of the web, detailing core elements like HTML, HTTP, TCP/IP, URIs, and SGML parsers. At first, I was overwhelmed by the amount of new terminology and protocols being introduced. Reading about these terms wasn’t the real challenge — it was understanding how they worked together as a system. It felt like being thrown into algebra without learning basic arithmetic. Like you know in Maths, you are first taught that 2 + 2 = 4 this foundation of addition later helps one understand why 4/2 = 2. I kept wishing there was a beginner-friendly structure that could map out the relationships between these parts: for example, how HTML structures the content, HTTP helps transmit it, and URIs tell you where to find it.

After stepping away and revisiting the article later, something shifted. I started connecting the dots, recognizing that the web isn’t a single tool, but a network of interdependent components. TCP/IP ensures devices can talk to each other, HTTP carries the requests and responses, and URIs act like coordinates to locate documents across the web. Even the GET, POST, and PUT methods began to make more sense as mechanisms for interacting with servers. Revisiting the article helped me realize that understanding the web isn’t about memorizing definitions. It’s about grasping how everything works together. And once I started seeing those connections, the reading stopped feeling so foreign and started feeling foundational.

SECTION 3: The History of Flash by Jay Hoffman

READING 2 FLASH I read an article, Flash And Its History On The Web, which unpacks the history behind FuturespLASH (FLASH) along with its evolution. Initially, FLASH was a FutureSplash Animator that was developed by FutureWave Software in the mid-1990s to be used as an animation tool for pen computing devices. In late 1996, Macromedia bought FutureSplash and rebranded it as FLASH. Software's prevalence picked up when it introduced the feature to stream compressed video content directly within web browsers. This feature would become the foundational blocks of how YouTube came to be.

TIMELINES Next I read a website that breaks down that timeline of the history ofthe web between 2002 and 2017. This timeline was interesting because it revealed how tech companies shape the industry not just through innovation but through carefully timed decisions that reshape the competitive landscape. iPhone strategically designed mobile devices that were released to not need the FLASH function. This forced web developers to transition to HTML5, which was more open and efficient but also happened to weaken Adobe's grip on web content.
