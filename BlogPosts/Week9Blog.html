Digital Divide Widens: Maria Tataki & Dimitris Glynos

In the article, Digital Divide Widens, Tataki and Glynos address the effects of the Covid-19 pandemic by emphasizing how this period magnified existing digital inequalities together with the layers that come with this digital divide. The core argument that is focused on in the article is realising that digital access should no longer be a luxury but really a fundamental right exhausted to everyone. The argument has become important because the pandemic has highlighted the cost of digital exclusion. The article reveals that people who come from lower-income households usually suffer the most when it comes to receiving accessibility.

But I think a further analysis into the situation is realising that accessibility is only the tip of the iceberg, assuming that ‘access alone is enough’ - is a problem! Having a device or an internet connection does not mean one can meaningfully participate in digital life, as Nanjira Sambuli said we need to “[dissect] the analog and the digital aspects of political, social, cultural, economic dimensions in society today… to ensure that we do not widen inequalities through digital technologies” (Sambuli 2019). In other words, there are more factors that play into the existence of the digital divide. The systemic structures need to interrogate whether the people receiving digital access also have digital literacy (Can users critically engage with content, avoid misinformation, or protect their privacy?) or even cultural relevance (Is the content accessible in one’s language or aligned with their local realities?). Over and above this, is also interrogating the metrics used, it is acknowledged that “830 million [students] do not have access to computers” (Tataki and Glynos 2020) but are we focusing too much on infrastructure, focusing more on people having devices, the correct bandwidth and not enough on long-term strategies like curriculum reform, digital training, and sustainable access.

While the article briefly acknowledges how girls and students in Sub-Saharan Africa are more disadvantaged, it could go further in using an intersectional lens. There are even more different aspects of identity (gender, race, age, disability, geographic location, or refugee status) that come into play to heighten the digital divide. For instance, a girl living in a rural area of Kenya faces gender bias, she could lack privacy at home to study online, or may face more chores during lockdowns. But also, she faces the infrastructural neglect and potentially linguistic barriers when accessing content online. A further step I took was looking into Kositany-Buckner’s article, Digital inclusion, his work helped deepen the understanding of how these overlapping disadvantages function digitally. But to go back to the article, it speaks of the Egyptian government handing out free SIM cards to students and free data plans, but does not question the sustainability of these measures. Emergency responses may bridge the gap momentarily, but what happens once these schemes end? A critical engagement here would raise concerns about whether these reactive interventions are setting up long-term infrastructure, or if they simply reinforce a cycle of dependence without structural reform.

Another aspect of the article is drawing attention to workers who cannot afford to work from home, but more critique is needed on how the digital divide is linked to poor paid working conditions. Many essential workers—such as domestic workers, security guards, and delivery drivers—are not only physically present during the pandemic but are often excluded from digital services like banking apps, online health consultations, or job listings that are migrating online. Which reinforces the idea that the digital divide is also tied to a class divide, we have what is termed the “digital underclass” that is permanently sidelined from full participation in the digital economy.

So all in all, access should not just be about consumption; it should include the ability to contribute to and shape the digital world. The article focuses on education and telework, but leaves out how digital divides affect civic participation, cultural production, and activism. Marginalized communities need platforms where they can express, document, and organise. The key message is emphasising that alot more can be done, a lot more should be done to bridge the digital divide.
