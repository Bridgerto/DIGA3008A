From First Push to Flash: A Journey Through the Web’s Past and My Digital Future.
Week 1 Blog

Published by: Reneilwe Thobejane

Posted: 18th February, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa
Lasted updated: 20th May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa

When I think about how I want to approach justice and ethics in my artistic and programming work, I know it’s not just about what I create, but how and why I create it. I’ve come to realise that every decision I make, what tools I use, who I design for, what stories I tell, what I choose to leave out, carries values. Whether I’m making an interactive piece, designing a game, or coding a system, I want to be intentional about those choices.

One reading that really stuck with me is Values at Play by Flanagan, Howe, and Nissenbaum (2005). They argue that games and interactive systems are never value-neutral. Instead of pretending that technology is “just a tool,” they encourage designers to think about the social and moral impact of their work. That made me think a lot about how I approach my own projects, especially since I’m someone who wants to mix art, culture, and tech in meaningful ways.

For me, it starts with awareness. I try to ask myself, What values are already showing up in my work, even unintentionally? Am I reinforcing harmful stereotypes? Am I making something only accessible to people with fast internet or expensive devices? If I’m designing something interactive, I want to think about who’s being represented and who’s being left out.

One of the things the authors suggest is thinking about “value-sensitive design,” which means including values like inclusivity, fairness, and empathy right from the start, not as an afterthought. I like the idea that you can design systems that reflect care, dignity, and community, just as much as you can design ones that are efficient or flashy.

In my art practice, I’m drawn to exploring themes like identity, inequality, and the lived experiences of people in underrepresented communities. That means I need to be thoughtful about how I frame those stories. Am I telling someone else’s story without their voice? Am I making assumptions based on my own worldview? These are tough questions, but they’re necessary.

On the programming side, justice also means thinking about data, where it comes from, who collected it, and what’s being done with it. I don’t want to build systems that rely on biased algorithms or exploit people’s information just to make something “work.” I think it’s okay to slow down and question the purpose of what I’m building. Who benefits? Who might be harmed?

Overall, I want to make work that reflects care and responsibility. I don’t always have the answers, but I think the first step is staying open and honest about the impact of my choices. Justice and ethics aren’t a box to tick off, they’re part of the entire process. I want my creative work to ask questions, hold space for different voices, and help build something better, even in small ways.
