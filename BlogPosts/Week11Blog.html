Article title

Week 11 Blog

Published by: Reneilwe Thobejane

Posted: 05th May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa
Last updated: 23rd May, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa

In her article Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why the Internet Needs African Feminists and Feminisms, Sheena Magenya provides a powerful account of how African feminist activists have come together to reimagine and rework the internet as a space of inclusion, justice, and radical presence. The piece centers around the 2019 Making a Feminist Internet in Africa (MFIAfrica) gathering, hosted by the Association for Progressive Communications Women’s Rights Programme (APC WRP), which brought together over 40 African feminist thinkers, artists, and digital activists. Against the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic and its exposure of digital inequalities, Magenya emphasizes that feminist internet-making is not merely a theoretical or symbolic pursuit. It is a critical response to systemic and overlapping forms of marginalization within digital space.

The article begins with the context of MFIAfrica, situating it within a lineage of global feminist internet-making efforts dating back to 2014. While previous gatherings occurred in South America, Eastern Europe, and Asia, this was the first of its kind held specifically for and by African feminists. Magenya argues that such a space is vital because African voices remain underrepresented or misrepresented online, and the design, governance, and usage of the internet still mirror colonial, capitalist, and patriarchal structures. In this way, her methodology, which is bringing together diverse African feminist voices in one room, is not just participatory but deeply reflective, producing a kind of situated feminist knowledge that is collective, intersectional, and grounded in lived experience.

A central theme in Magenya’s article is the entanglement between digital inequalities and broader social injustices. She asserts that the internet, much like the offline world, has become a site of gender-based violence, surveillance, and erasure. In African contexts, these dynamics are exacerbated by poor access to technology, underdeveloped digital infrastructure, and laws that do not yet recognize online abuse as real violence. This aligns closely with Nanjira Sambuli’s argument in What is Digital Equality?, where she defines digital inequality as a modern civil rights issue, insisting that equitable participation in digital spaces is essential for full democratic engagement. Sambuli notes that access is not just about connectivity but about meaningful use—having the resources, knowledge, and cultural safety to navigate and shape digital space.

Magenya pushes back against the dominant narrative that sees Africa solely as a beneficiary of Western technology. She critiques the repeated framing of African technological innovation as reactive, shaped only by crisis or scarcity (such as responses to famine, disease, or conflict), rather than imaginative or playful. This dominant “single story,” as she invokes from Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie, erases African creativity, particularly that of women, queer people, and disabled individuals. In a world where mainstream media narratives are still largely produced by Western outlets, African feminists’ presence online becomes an act of defiance, a way to rewrite stories, assert visibility, and build new futures.

This notion of reclaiming narrative and access is echoed in the work of Cheptoo Kositany-Buckner, who emphasizes the importance of community-centered internet access. She argues that access should be treated as a public good, where communities not only consume content but are equipped to create and govern their own networks. Similarly, in the article Digital Divide Widens by Maria Tataki and Dimitris Glynos, the authors show how the COVID-19 pandemic laid bare the severe disparities in internet access, particularly in the Global South. With work, education, and healthcare increasingly moving online, those without reliable access are excluded from essential services and democratic participation.

What distinguishes Magenya’s article, however, is its hopeful, grounded tone. Rather than being purely diagnostic, it is aspirational, envisioning what a feminist internet could look like. This internet is not only safe and inclusive but also vibrant, joyful, and sensual. She calls for a space with “more glitter and ochre,” full of body-positive content, queer African love stories, makeup tutorials for gay boys, and fashion for fat Black women. This framing of joy and play as resistance is particularly powerful. In a digital ecosystem often characterized by violence and toxicity, imagining pleasure as a feminist practice becomes radical.

Yet, Magenya does not romanticize the road ahead. She acknowledges that structural inequalities remain deeply entrenched, and that more gatherings, more solidarity, and more action are needed. Her conclusion—that the feminist internet is no longer just desirable but crucial—resonates with the urgent calls made by Sambuli and others. The pandemic has shown us that digital inclusion is a lifeline, not a luxury. Without intentional efforts to build feminist, African-centered digital spaces, the internet risks becoming a place of deepening inequality, not liberation.

In reflecting critically, one could argue that while the MFIAfrica gathering was a powerful intervention, such in-person events may struggle to effect change at scale without accompanying structural policy shifts. There remains a gap between grassroots feminist digital activism and national/international digital policy-making, which is still largely shaped by governments and corporate interests. Bridging this gap would require not just activism, but also advocacy, coalition-building with regulators, and involvement in tech design and governance processes.

Nevertheless, the significance of Magenya’s work lies in its insistence that African feminists belong at the center of digital futures. She frames storytelling, pleasure, and visibility not as extras, but as foundations for justice. As the internet continues to shape how we live, love, and resist, making it feminist—making it African—is not just a dream. It is a political necessity.
