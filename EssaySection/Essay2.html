The Illusion of Neutrality: Mapping the Gulf of America on a Digital Battleground

Author: Reneilwe Thobejane
Posted: 13th June, 2025 Johannesburg, South Africa

In early February 2025, Google Maps took a controversial step and officialised the renaming of the Gulf of Mexico to the Gulf of America. This change only affects people using the app in the United States of America, those in Mexico will not see the change while everyone else around the world will see: Gulf of Mexico (Gulf of America). Google took this action in accordance with an executive order signed by President Donald Trump, which called for the restoring of names that “honor American greatness”. As part of the same initiative, Trump took a step further and declared February 9th as “Gulf of America Day” (Davies, 2025), encouraging the public to commemorate the change nationwide. In response, the Mexican government disapproved of this action, arguing the US had no legal right to independently follow through with the renaming. The Mexican president, Claudia Sheinbaum, even wrote a formal letter to ask Google to reconsider the name change.

The company responded by explaining that the move is part of a “longstanding practice” of following name changes when updated by official government sources. As of today, the change remains in place. This incident highlights how digital platforms like Google Maps are not neutral tools, they can be used to reflect and enforce geopolitical agendas. What appears to be a name change reveals layered issues around power, representation and territorial narratives in the digital age. The map becomes a symbolic battleground where geopolitical power plays out. This conflict around the renaming reflects ways in which digital colonialism continues to play out today. Powerful nations along with their companies continue to assert influence and reshape narratives by claiming space even on digital platforms. Thus revealing the complex relationship between the internet, geopolitics and digital colonialism.

Google Map renaming the Gulf of Mexico may seem like a neutral interface change, meanwhile this shows how digital platforms can become extensions of power from the government that reinforce nationalistic agendas. In this event, the executive order signed by Trump in order to "honor American Greatness" frames the renaming as a patriotic act, he even “said … that the name Gulf of America had a “beautiful ring. It's appropriate. And Mexico has to stop allowing millions of people to pour into our country” (Milmo 2025). This statement shows that the name change has a political agenda. Not to mention that Trump was not in office when the name appeared on Google Maps, the change was still accepted because it was added to an official U.S. database called the Geographic Names Information System (GNIS). Google explained that the renaming is following official government data. Which highlights a deeper issue surrounding legal systems and digital platforms always helping to spread only one country's view over others. This shows that the power to define digital space is unevenly distributed, often favoring dominant countries like the United States. As Mark Graham (2014) argues in Internet Geographies, the internet is not a flat or neutral space, it is structured by global inequalities in data production, representation, and visibility. Platforms like Google Maps source most of their naming data from institutions in the Global North, meaning their digital geographies often reflect Western political realities and priorities.

Additionally, the Mexican government disagreed with the name change to a body of water that is shared internationally. President Sheinbaum highlighted the “...[Law of the Seas by] United Nations Convention [which] dictates that an individual country's sovereign territory only extends up to 12 nautical miles out from the coastline” (Davies 2025) meaning that the US could not legally change the Gulf's name. Yet the name change still appeared for users in the US and partially from everyone around the world, showing how platforms can be used as geopolitical instruments through shaping people's perception of the internet and the real world. Graham supports this notion by affirming that those with the infrastructure and resources to generate and formalise data are more likely to have their narratives digitally amplified. This therefore emphasises that digital spaces are not neutral, when paired with legal systems alongside influential tech companies, they can reshape digital representation of geography under the guise of government policies to favour and extend geopolitical power. Thus reflecting digital colonialism happening in real time.

Another way digital colonialism shows up is when digital platforms choose to continue systems of power by giving more weight to dominant countries' views while ignoring others. In this case, Google renaming the Gulf of Mexico to the Gulf of America is more than just a name change, it is a symbolic act of rewriting history. A powerful country, the US, using digital tools to rename a place with deep cultural and historical significance to another, becomes a form of epistemic erasure. This kind of renaming strips Mexico and its people of the right to define their own spaces and histories. As Birhane (2020) argues in Algorithmic Colonization of Africa, such practices reflect a larger pattern of epistemic control, where dominant states (from the Global North) determine what knowledge is seen as legitimate, worth preserving and relevant. Thus resulting in the reduction and ignoring or overwriting of local, contextual and indigenous ways of knowing. Which reflects what Birhane calls the "outsourcing of cognitive authority", where powerful institutions export their own frameworks of understanding while "dismissing the epistemologies of those being represented" (Birhane 2020).

Therefore showing how the current structure and operation of the digital ecosystem tends to prioritise or give more visibility to the narratives and interests of powerful countries, usually from the Global North. Which further suggests that digital spaces are not equal, this appearance of neutrality isn't accidental, it was encoded this way in order to frame technology as something that functions as a trusted global tool. While in actuality, it is an active participant in shaping reality, through embedding the values and worldviews of those who build and control them. This turns the global infrastructure into a stage for digital colonisation, just like colonial maps once defined territories according to imperial logics, today's digital maps do the same through determining who gets to name, define, and narrate the world. Birhane's work reminds us that these are not just technological issues, but deeply political and ethical ones about voice, dignity, and the right to self-representation.

Moreover, this renaming incident brings to attention how digital colonialism operates as part of a larger system of Neocolonialism. Now, powerful countries are using indirect means like technology, media and data to gain control over less powerful countries. In the earlier paragraph, I quoted Trump's comment, “Mexico has to stop allowing millions of people to pour into our country” (Milmo 2025), showing that the renaming of the Gulf of Mexico is not just a digital decision, it reflects a deeper political strategy. The name change is tied to a broader anti-immigrant and nationalistic agenda. Thus highlighting how digital spaces can reinforce systemic racism and discrimination under the guise of patriotism. To affirm this argument, Lutz's 2019 article on digital inequalities touches on the ways inequality goes beyond access, it's about how digital systems are used and what outcomes they produce. In this case, the renaming reflects control over digital representation, where the US narrative is algorithmically embedded into global platforms. The US deciding to rename a shared body of water without consulting Mexico is a symbolic act of cultural dominance. Which again emphasizes the orchestrated neutrality of digital platforms, they operate in the broader conversation of Neocolonialism.

Thus, this case study reveals how digital platforms (Google Maps in this case) are not passive tools but active participants in global power dynamics. The act of promoting the single ideologies of dominant countries shows their contribution to digital colonialism and reflects broader patterns of colonialism. The appearance of a simple technological decision actually carries deeper implications around political influence, cultural erasure and systemic inequality.
